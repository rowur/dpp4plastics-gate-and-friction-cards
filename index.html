Implementing Digital Product Passports for Technical Plastics in Electronic Products: Mapping Actor-Specific Data Requirements and Normative Frictions

Digital Gate and Friction Cards providing additional detail on lifecycle gates, actor-specific data elements, and normative frictions for DPP implementation in technical plastics used in electronic products.

About

These digital cards support the poster presentation by providing additional detail on lifecycle gates and normative frictions for DPP implementation in technical plastics used in electronic products.

How to read the cards

The gate logic follows the material flow along lifecycle gates, from upstream material supply to downstream EoL processing and recyclate qualification.

Gate Cards show selected actor-specific data elements, evidence requirements, reference-unit shifts and gate-level limitations. Friction Cards describe normative and operational tensions that affect practical DPP use across the value chain.

Separate pages list abbreviations, regulations and standards for orientation. References and detailed project context are provided in the DPP4Plastics project report.

Scope and limitations

The cards are analytical support material. They do not constitute a legal compliance checklist, certification scheme, standardized reference framework, or complete DPP implementation guide.

They simplify complex regulatory, technical and organisational relationships for discussion.

Feedback and discussion

Feedback and discussion are welcome, especially on missing actor-specific data elements, unclear gate transitions, relevant standards or evidence types, and additional frictions observed in practical DPP implementation.

G1 Material Supply
Upstream
7 data elements
Can this material batch be used, documented and transferred into component manufacturing?
Tap to expand data elements.
Gate description
At the material-supply gate, the supplier establishes material identity, composition, technical specification and baseline compliance for a material batch or delivery lot. The function is to make material-level claims and evidence traceable for subsequent manufacturing while protecting confidential formulation details beyond what is needed.
Operational decisions supported
• Accept material batch,
• qualify supplier,
• check baseline compliance,
• release batch for processing,
• document custody.
Actors
Material supplier, including virgin polymer producer, recycled material supplier and raw material producer;
Reference unit / Digital artefact
Material batch or delivery lot /
Material DPP
DPP record function
• Record material-batch identity and evidence,
• link supplier, grade, composition and compliance data,
• make material records available to downstream actors,
• map to transfomration or equivalent traceability record.
Regulatory anchors
• (ESPR),
• REACH,
• CLP,
• RoHS
Standard / technical anchor
• ISO 22095 if origin, certified input or mass-balance claims are made,
• EN 15343, applicable with recycled-content claim
Key limitation
Formulation and additive data may be commercially sensitive, although downstream actors need hazard- and recyclability-relevant information.

G3 End Product
Upstream
8 data elements
Can this product be placed on the market with sufficient product-level evidence and downstream intake information?

Gate description
At the end-product gate, the OEM or actor placing the product on the market consolidates component, material and conformity information into the market-facing product representation. This creates the primary product-level DPP and links upstream evidence to product claims and downstream intake information.
Operational decisions supported
• Declare product conformity,
• issue product DPP or equivalent product record,
• place product on the market,
• support EPR / WEEE obligations,
• provide downstream intake information.
Actors
OEM, brand owner or actor placing the product on the market;
Reference unit / Digital artefact
Product item,
serialised product,
product model /
End-product DPP
DPP record function
• Issue the primary market-facing product record,
• link product item or model to bill of materials, components, material records, conformity evidence and downstream handling information,
• support later collection and pre-processing as intake information source.
Regulatory anchors
• ESPR,
• REACH,
• RoHS support,
• WEEE,
• CE-related regimes
such as EMC, RED or LVD where applicable
Standard / technical anchor
• EN IEC 63000,
• EN 50693 if LCA / PCF information is included,
• ISO 22095 for transferred material claims,
• EN 15343 as inherited evidence for recycled-plastic claims,
• UL 94 – if required
• EN 4555x series
Key limitation
Market-facing compliance information is not identical to the operational data needed for sorting, treatment and requalification at End-of-Life.

G4 Collection
Downstream / Product-to-waste transition
2 data elements
Can this product or intake stream be identified, documented and assigned to a collection batch?
Tap to expand data elements.
Gate description
At collection, products enter the End-of-Life chain and are aggregated into collection batches. The function shifts from product-level information provision to intake documentation, custody recording and linkage between readable product identifiers and batch-level waste records.
Operational decisions supported
• Accept intake,
• scan or read identifiers where possible,
• create collection batch,
• document custody,
• route to pre-processing,
• support WEEE / EPR reporting.
Actors
Collector, Producer Responsibility Organisation (PRO), take-back or logistics operator, collection point or retail take-back system.
Reference unit / Digital artefact
Collection batch,
container or intake lot /
End-product DPP as intake source and PRBL created
DPP record function
• Link readable product identifiers to collection batches where possible,
• initiate PRBL or equivalent downstream batch record,
• record aggregation, intake, mass and custody transfer,
• maintain the product-level DPP as intake information source rather than downstream identity anchor.
Regulatory anchors
• WEEE Directive,
• Extended Producer Responsibility (EPR),
• Waste Framework Directive (WFD),
• Waste Shipment Directive (WSD)
Standard / technical anchor
• ISO 22095 if collection batches are linked to later claims,
• EN 15343 not primary at collection stage,
• CWA 18186 / PRBL if batch records are used
Key limitation
DPP-enabled and non-DPP products will coexist, and physical identifiers may not be read or readable at intake.

G5 Pre-processing
Downstream
2 data elements

Gate description
At pre-processing, collected products or batches are dismantled, depolluted and separated into technically and legally manageable sub-streams. The gate preserves treatment-relevant evidence, records removals and screening outcomes, and maintains an auditable bridge from collection batches to downstream recycling outputs.
Operational decisions supported
• Depollute and dismantle,
• remove hazardous or value-relevant components,
• split collection batch into treatment sub-streams,
• document treatment,
• route outputs to sorting.
Actors
WEEE treatment operator, pre-treatment operator, dismantling facility or recycler.
Reference unit / Digital artefact
Treatment batch, sub-stream, sub-batch or process output /
PRBL updated
DPP record function
• Update PRBL or equivalent batch record as collection batches are dismantled, depolluted or split into treatment sub-streams,
• use product or component DPP data where still available to guide treatment,
• shift continuity increasingly to process and sub-stream records.
Regulatory anchors
• WEEE Directive,
• POPs Regulation,
• REACH,
• WFD,
• WSD
Standard / technical anchor
• EN 50625 for WEEE treatment obligations,
• ISO 22095 for preserving traceability across dismantling and fraction formation,
• EN 15343 conditional if documented plastic fractions feed recycled-plastic production,
• CWA 18186 / PRBL if batch records are used
Key limitation
Product identity begins to break down as products are opened, dismantled, mixed or size-reduced.

G6 Sorting
Downstream / Waste-to-Product transition
2 data elements
Can this mixed stream be sorted into fractions that are reliable enough for recycling decisions?
Tap to expand data elements.
Gate description
At sorting, mixed sub-streams are converted into polymer or application-relevant fractions. Product-level identity becomes operationally secondary, while fraction identity, sorting results, contamination indicators, substance-risk information and chain-of-custody records become decisive for recycling decisions.
Operational decisions supported
• Separate polymers,
• classify fractions,
• control contamination,
• route or reject fractions,
• document quality and screening results,
• prepare suitable fractions for compounding.
Actors
Sorting operator, sorting facility or recycler.
Reference unit / Digital artefact
Sorted fraction, polymer fraction, application-relevant batch or sorting output stream. /
PRBL updated
DPP record function
• Record transformation from mixed sub-streams to sorted fractions,
• link fraction IDs to sorting, screening and quality results,
• update PRBL or equivalent batch record from sub-stream to sorted fraction,
• preserve batch/fraction continuity after product-level identity is physically lost.
Regulatory anchors
• POPs Regulation,
• REACH,
• RoHS restriction relevance,
• End-of-Waste considerations under WFD Article 6 where sorting evidence supports later qualification
Standard / technical anchor
• ISO 22095 for traceability from mixed streams to sorted fractions,
• EN 15343 where sorted polymer fractions enter recycled-plastic traceability,
• PRE-1000-type screening where used,
• CWA 18186 / PRBL if batch records are used
Key limitation
Item-level DPP logic becomes insufficient for fraction-level decisions once physical identity is lost.

Downstream
7 data elements

Gate description
At compounding, sorted fractions are converted into recycled polymer or compound batches that re-enter production as material inputs. The gate establishes a new material-level evidence package for the recyclate, including batch identity, quality data, test reports, recycled-content information and qualification limits.
Operational decisions supported
• Qualify recycled output batch,
• test and release regranulate or compound,
• substantiate recycled-content and quality claims,
• support product re-entry and procurement decisions for recycled plastics.
Actors
Recycled material producer, compounder or recycler;
Reference unit / Digital artefact
Recycled polymer batch, regranulate batch, compound batch or recyclate output batch. /
PRBL finalised
Recycled material DPP
DPP record function
• Create a material-level record / DPP for the recycled output batch,
• link input fractions, PRBL or equivalent batch history, compounding process, CoA, DQL evidence and final QC results,
• support re-entry claims and transaction records for handover.
Regulatory anchors
• WFD / End-of-Waste,
• REACH,
• CLP,
• RoHS support,
• POPs Regulation
Standard / technical anchor
• EN 15343 for recycled-plastics traceability and recycled-content calculation,
• ISO 22095 for chain-of-custody, mixing and claim allocation,
• EN 18065 DQL / product data sheet,
• CWA 18186 / PRBL if batch records are used
Key limitation
Recyclate claims require new batch-level testing and evidence, they cannot simply inherit upstream product claims.

Friction Cards

The friction cards summarise normative and operational tensions that affect practical DPP use across the value chain. They focus on core tensions, consequences for DPP use, and governance implications.
F1 Product identity and Batch reality
Tap to expand friction details.
Core tension
Product-level records refer to identifiable products, while End-of-Life operations increasingly operate with collection batches, sub-streams, sorted fractions and recyclate output batches.
Consequence for DPP use
Item-level identity can support intake and routing, but it cannot remain the central continuity logic after aggregation, dismantling, shredding or sorting.
DPP / governance implication
Link product-level DPPs at intake to PRBL records or equivalent downstream batch, fraction and output-batch records. Recycled output batches should be represented through a new material or recyclate-level record.
F2 Compliance evidence and Recycling data
Tap to expand friction details.
Core tension
Upstream compliance evidence is generated mainly for product, chemical or market-placement purposes, while recyclers need operational information for sorting, screening, treatment and secondary-material qualification.
Consequence for DPP use
Legal or technical documentation may exist but still be insufficient for routing, depollution, sorting or recyclate approval.
DPP / governance implication
Separate compliance evidence from recycling-relevant evidence. Link both to the correct reference unit, evidence basis, issuer and intended decision context.
F3 Confidentiality and Need-to-know
Tap to expand friction details.
Core tension
Formulation, additive and supplier information can be commercially sensitive, while downstream actors need selected information for safe and effective recycling.
Consequence for DPP use
Too little disclosure reduces usefulness for sorting and qualification; excessive disclosure creates intellectual-property and liability concerns.
DPP / governance implication
Use layered access, restricted evidence, hazard flags and need-to-know disclosure instead of full formulation transparency.
F4 Inherited claims and Requalified recyclate
Tap to expand friction details.
Core tension
Material-, component- or product-level claims cannot automatically be transferred to recycled fractions or recyclate output batches after physical transformation and mixing.
Consequence for DPP use
Recyclate claims require new batch-level evidence, such as screening results, material tests and output-batch qualification.
DPP / governance implication
Treat recyclate claims as new evidence-backed claims. Link them to batch tests, certificates of analysis, screening results and Data Quality Level documentation where applicable.
F5 DPP-enabled and Legacy streams
Tap to expand friction details.
Core tension
DPP-enabled products will coexist with legacy, non-readable or non-DPP waste streams for a long time.
Consequence for DPP use
End-of-Life actors must handle digitally identifiable products and anonymous bulk streams in parallel, which can increase documentation and process-control burden.
DPP / governance implication
Define fallback identification, batch documentation and transitional governance. Do not assume that all waste inputs will be digitally readable.
F6 Standard models and Fragmented actor systems
Tap to expand friction details.
Core tension
Formal DPP models must operate across heterogeneous enterprise systems, lab records, quality assurance systems, recycler logs and platform-specific tools.
Consequence for DPP use
Data exchange may remain manual, lossy or non-machine-readable even if a formal data model exists.
DPP / governance implication
Use shared identifiers, controlled vocabularies, sector-specific profiles and mappings between existing operational systems and DPP data objects.
F7 Chemical safety and Circularity objectives
Tap to expand friction details.
Core tension
Chemical safety requirements, legacy substances, restricted additives and persistent pollutants can conflict with recycling and circularity objectives.
Consequence for DPP use
Technically recyclable fractions may still require exclusion, additional screening, downcycling or rejection because of substance-related risk.
DPP / governance implication
Combine upstream hazard or restriction flags with downstream batch- and fraction-specific validation, including screening and test evidence where needed.
F8 Status transitions and Changing evidence requirements
Tap to expand friction details.
Core tension
A plastic part may move from product status to waste status and later to a recyclate or secondary material. At each transition, the relevant evidence, responsibilities and decision criteria change.
Consequence for DPP use
Information sufficient for product compliance or market placement may not be sufficient for waste treatment, End-of-Waste assessment or secondary-material qualification.
DPP / governance implication
Document the transition from product item to waste stream, fraction and recyclate output batch. Clarify which actor provides which evidence at each status change and which record supports re-entry into production.
F9 Recycled-content accounting and Chain-of-custody evidence
Tap to expand friction details.
Core tension
Recycled-content claims depend on the chain-of-custody logic used, such as physical segregation, controlled blending, mass balance or comparable attribution approaches.
Consequence for DPP use
A recycled-content percentage is not meaningful unless the attribution method, input shares, calculation basis and evidence scope are clear.
DPP / governance implication
State the chain-of-custody model, calculation method, attribution period and supporting evidence. Link claims to batch records, supplier evidence and recycled-output qualification.
